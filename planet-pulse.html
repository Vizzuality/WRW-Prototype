Forest loss since 2000(Hansen, UMD, Google, USGS, NASA 2013)

This dataset uses satellite imagery to measure the area of tree cover loss annually since the year 2000, as well as measuring total tree cover in the year 2000, and average annual tree cover gain since 2000.

"Hansen, M. C., P. V. Potapov, R. Moore, M. Hancher, S. A. Turubanova, A. Tyukavina, D. Thau, S. V. Stehman, S. J. Goetz, T. R. Loveland, A. Kommareddy, A. Egorov, L. Chini, C. O. Justice, and J. R. G. Townshend. 2013. “High-Resolution Global Maps of 21st-Century Forest Cover Change.” Science 342 (15 November): 850–53. Data available online from: http://earthenginepartners.appspot.com/science-2013-global-forest.

Hansen, M. C., P. V. Potapov, R. Moore, M. Hancher, S. A. Turubanova, A. Tyukavina, D. Thau, S. V. Stehman, S. J. Goetz, T. R. Loveland, A. Kommareddy, A. Egorov, L. Chini, C. O. Justice, and J. R. G. Townshend. 2013. “Hansen/UMD/Google/USGS/NASA Tree Cover Loss and Gain Area.” University of Maryland, Google, USGS, and NASA."

Deforestation alerts (Forma)(WRI, CGD)

This dataset provides alerts twice every month of areas where tree cover loss is likely to have occurred. These alerts are generated through analysis of satellite images for the world’s humid tropical forests.

Hammer, Dan, Robin Kraft, and David Wheeler. 2013. “FORMA Alerts.” World Resources Institute and Center for Global Development.

Protected Areas(IUCN, UNEP-WCMC)

This dataset shows the location of terrestrial and marine protected areas around the world.

IUCN and UNEP-WCMC, The World Database on Protected Areas (WDPA), Cambridge, UK: UNEP-WCMC. Available at: www.protectedplanet.net.

Fires(NASA)

NASA's Fire Information for Resource Management System (FIRMS) data delivers near real-time, MODIS-derived hotspots and fire locations for the past seven days.

NASA FIRMS. “NASA Fire Information for Resource Management System (FIRMS).” earthdata.nasa.gov/data/near-real-time-data/firms.

MODIS-Derived Global Fire Products. Land Remote Sensing and Global Environmental Change. B. Ramachandran, C. O. Justice and M. J. Abrams, Springer New York. 11: 661-679. doi:10.1007/978-1-4419-6749-7_29.

Drought

Water is a fundamental resource. Globally, 70 percent of freshwater use is for agriculture, 22 percent for industry, and 8 percent for household activities. Understanding how this resource changes over time can help us ensure robust economy and sustainable food supply for all.

Forest

Forest ecosystems also play a critical role in stabilizing the climate; providing food, water, wood products, and medicines; and supporting much of the world’s biodiversity. Though an estimated 30% of potential forest cover has been lost, we can still productively and sustainably manage the world’s remaining forests.

Displayed layers

  • Deforestation alerts (Forma)
  • Forest loss since 2000 (UMD/Google)

Flooding

Flooding and other extreme weather events are one of the most direct ways in which a changing climate will affect our lives. Tens of millions of people are affected by flooding each year. The future of flooding depends on how we choose to develop our rivers and coasts, and how we affect the Earth’s climate.
